## TL;DR
Doting is for education and supportive communication habits, not emergency, medical, or crisis care. If immediate danger is present, contact emergency services right away.[^1][^2]

## Why this matters
In hard moments, people need the right level of support.
A prompt, gesture, or card can help everyday connection, but they are not substitutes for urgent professional help.
Clear boundaries protect everyone: couples, families apart, children, caregivers, and people under acute stress.[^3]

## Do this now
- [ ] Save local emergency numbers in your phone.
- [ ] If you are in the U.S., save **988** in contacts.
- [ ] Share your preferred emergency contacts with a trusted person.
- [ ] Keep one brief support card template ready for check-ins.

## Your Doting Prompt
“Act as a relationship coach (non-clinical). I want a supportive check-in message for <person>.
Relationship: <partner/family member>.
Context: <stressful period; we need calm communication>.
Constraints: <short message, low energy, no advice overload>.
Tone: <grounded, reassuring>.
Give me: (1) one 2-minute supportive gesture, (2) one text message draft, (3) one short customizable card message, (4) one follow-up question that checks safety and practical needs.”

## One Small Gesture (doable today)
**Gesture:** “Support contact prep”  
- **Time:** 5 minutes  
- **Cost:** $0  
- **How:** Add emergency contact and crisis lines to favorites.
- **Make it easier:** Ask one trusted person to do this with you on a call.

## Copy/paste scripts
**Text message**  
“I care about you and want to make sure you’re supported today. Do you need practical help, a call, or quiet company?”

**Card message (52 words)**  
“I’m thinking of you and I care about your safety and wellbeing. You do not have to carry everything alone. If things feel overwhelming, we can take one next step together—reach out, ask for help, and keep it simple. I’m here to support, not pressure.”

## If this feels awkward
- If you worry about overstepping, ask consent: “Can I share support options?”
- If someone declines help, keep the door open and check back.
- If risk feels immediate, prioritize safety actions over long conversations.

## For couples
Use clear language in conflict: “I want us safe and respectful. Let’s pause and return at <time>.”

## For families
Share crisis contacts across caregivers so support does not depend on one person.

## When to get extra support
If someone feels unsafe, threatened, or in immediate danger, contact local emergency services.  
If you are in the U.S. and in crisis, call, text, or chat **988**: https://988lifeline.org/.[^1][^2]
