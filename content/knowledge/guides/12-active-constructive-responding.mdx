## TL;DR
How you respond to good news matters. Active, constructive responses are linked with better well-being and relationship outcomes.[^1][^2]

## Why this matters
Many relationships are maintained in tiny moments, not big talks.
When someone shares good news and gets a flat response, connection can shrink.
When they get curiosity and celebration, the moment expands.
Research on capitalization suggests sharing positive events can improve individual and relational outcomes when responses are engaged and supportive.[^1]
This is useful for couples and for families apart who rely on quick digital check-ins.[^3]

## Do this now
- [ ] When you hear good news, pause and face it directly.
- [ ] Start with clear enthusiasm (“That’s great news!”).
- [ ] Ask one specifics question (“What part are you proudest of?”).
- [ ] Reflect impact (“You worked hard for this.”).
- [ ] Save the message as a card template for next time.

## Your Doting Prompt
“Act as a relationship coach (non-clinical). I want to strengthen connection with <person>.
Relationship: <partner/family member>.
Context: <they shared good news and I want to respond better>.
Constraints: <2-5 minutes, text-only, low mental bandwidth>.
Tone: <warm, upbeat, sincere>.
Give me: (1) one 2-minute celebratory gesture, (2) one text response draft, (3) one short customizable card message, (4) one follow-up question that helps them savor the moment.”

## One Small Gesture (doable today)
**Gesture:** “Savor for 90 seconds”  
- **Time:** 2 minutes  
- **Cost:** $0  
- **How:** Ask one follow-up question and listen for 90 seconds without redirecting to your own story.
- **Make it easier:** If live conversation is hard, send one celebratory text + one curious question.

## Copy/paste scripts
**Text message**  
“That is awesome news. What part feels best to you right now? I want to celebrate it with you.”

**Voice-note outline**
- “I’m genuinely excited for you.”
- “What was the turning point?”
- “I noticed your effort in…”
- “How should we mark this, even in a small way?”

## If this feels awkward
- If enthusiasm feels unnatural, use gratitude + curiosity.
- If you feel envy, acknowledge it privately and still show support.
- If timing is bad, send a brief “I saw this and I’m glad for you.”
- If you accidentally minimized it, repair fast.

## For couples
Create a “good news first” ritual at dinner or bedtime: each person shares one win and gets an active response.[^2]

## For families
For kids, siblings, or elders, echo the effort behind the win, not only the result. That keeps encouragement grounded.[^4]

## When to get extra support
If someone feels unsafe, threatened, or in immediate danger, contact local emergency services.  
If you are in the U.S. and in crisis, call, text, or chat **988**: https://988lifeline.org/.[^5][^6]
